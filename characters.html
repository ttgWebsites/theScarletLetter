Characters

  • Hester Prynne
  • The protagonist of the book, the story tells of her struggles living in her community after she is convicted of adultery
  • She is forced to wear a scarlet red letter “A” on her chest to signify her adultery
  • Her husband, Chillingworth, sent her to America yet didn’t follow her
  • She had an affair with the towns minister, Dimmesdale, with which a child was born
  • She is a resilient and passionate women, and endures many years of persecution for her acts
  • Pearl
  • Hester’s daughter
  • She is very young in the novel. She is a moody girl who likes to push her luck and do mischievous things
  • She also can perceive things that others can not
  • She discovers the truth about Dimmesdale and her mother on her own
  • The townspeople think she is not human and that her father is the devil, when it’s just that she is wise beyond her years
  • Roger Chillingworth
  • Hester’s husband in disguise, he without Hester’s knowledge, finally comes to America
  • He arrives and see’s Hester in the scaffold, and realizes her adulteress acts
  • Roger chooses to stay in Boston in disguise even though his wife cheated on him
  • His plan is to disguise himself as a doctor and find Hester’s lover and torment him
  • Arthur Dimmesdale
  • The Reverend of the community church
  • Has an affair with Hester in which an illegitimate child is born
  • He puts himself through torment after his act, ultimately developing a heart condition due to his stress
  • Beloved by his community for his powerful sermons, and selflessness
  • Governor Bellingham
  • Wealthy, old gentleman who consults often with the other town fathers
  • Though the Governor of an a American community, he acts much like a European aristocrat
  • His decisions are often swayed by Dimmesdale
  • Mistress Hibbins
  • Widowed, she lives with her brother Governor Bellingham
  • She is a witch, who often roams the forest at night and rides with the “black man”
  • Reverend Mr. John Wilson
  • An elder clergyman, from Boston
  • He is the stereotype of Puritan reverends
  • He follows the strict rules of the community, yet is often swayed by Dimmesdale
  • His sermons are much different then Dimmesdale’s often discussing damnation, the suffering for sins, he also believes in harsh punishment for sinners
  • Narrator
  • The person telling the story found the story working in the Salem Custom-House 2 centuries after it took place
  • He tells Hester’s story in his own fictional way, even though it clashes with his Puritan beliefs he thinks the story should be told
